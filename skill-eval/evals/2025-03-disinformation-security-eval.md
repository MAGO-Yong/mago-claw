## What Was Done Well

1. **$25 million Hong Kong case**: Most vivid and immediately alarming example in the entire report set—establishes enterprise stakes instantly
2. **"Detection arms race" framing**: Correctly identifies why detection is not a long-term solution; provenance as alternative is intellectually sophisticated
3. **C2PA architecture explanation**: Clear, specific; trust chain concept is well-explained
4. **Seven attack vectors**: Comprehensive taxonomy that helps security teams scope their exposure
5. **"Trust But Verify" shift**: The communications protocol implications are underappreciated and well-stated

## What Was Done Poorly

1. **Detection accuracy figures**: "96% accuracy" for FakeCatcher needs context—accuracy on what test set? Older deepfakes? Latest models?
2. **Response time expectations**: No estimate of how quickly disinformation spreads vs. time to detect and respond
3. **Legal remedies section is thin**: When disinformation causes harm, what legal options exist? This is often the first question executives ask

## Improvement Suggestions

1. Add a "speed of disinformation" analysis—how quickly can fake content go viral vs. response time
2. Expand legal section: defamation, market manipulation law, platform liability for deepfakes
3. Add country-specific disinformation regulations (EU NetzDG, UK Online Safety Act, China regulations)
