# Total Experience (TX) — Research Report

**Year:** 2021 (Gartner announced October 2020), repeated 2022
**Topic Code:** 2021-02
**Research Date:** 2026-03-22

---

## Round 1: Foundational Research Memo

### 1. Topic Alignment

**Definition:** Total Experience (TX) is a business strategy that connects customer experience (CX), employee experience (EX), user experience (UX), and multiexperience (MX) into a unified, integrated whole. Rather than optimizing each experience domain in isolation, TX treats them as interdependent and designs them to reinforce each other.

Gartner's formulation: TX combines multiexperience (how people interact across different modalities—web, mobile, voice, AR/VR) with customer experience, employee experience, and user experience disciplines. The goal is that a positive employee experience enables better customer experiences, creating virtuous cycles.

**Adjacent and Related Concepts:**
- **CX (Customer Experience)**: One component of TX; the set of interactions a customer has with a brand
- **EX (Employee Experience)**: The employee-facing component; how employees interact with tools, culture, processes
- **UX (User Experience)**: Interface-level design for any digital touchpoint
- **MX (Multiexperience)**: How experiences span different interaction modalities (mobile, web, wearable, voice)
- **Total Quality Management**: Historical predecessor concept in operations
- **Service Design**: Design discipline focused on holistic service experiences
- **Digital Workplace**: Infrastructure enabling employee experience

**What TX is NOT:**
- Not just a CX program with employee consideration bolted on
- Not a technology—it's a strategy that requires technology enablement
- Not the same as omnichannel (which focuses on channel consistency, not experience integration)

---

### 2. Light Intake

**Why Gartner Featured This:**

COVID-19 radically disrupted traditional experience delivery. Organizations had to simultaneously:
- Shift customer interactions online (contactless retail, digital banking)
- Support remote/hybrid workforces
- Maintain service quality across new digital touchpoints

This created pressure to integrate the design of customer and employee digital experiences. Companies that had siloed CX and HR technology stacks found them misaligned.

**What Was Already Known:**
- Gallup research: Companies with high employee engagement see 20% higher sales and 21% higher profitability
- Forrester's CX Index had been tracking customer experience quality since 2007
- The "employee-first" hypothesis (happy employees → happy customers) was a management theory, less a technology strategy
- Gartner had introduced "multiexperience" as a 2020 trend—TX was explicitly building on that foundation

### 3. Foundational Research Findings

#### 3.1 Market Context

TX as a strategy sits at the intersection of multiple technology markets:
- **Digital experience platforms (DXP)**: $10.5 billion market in 2022 (Mordor Intelligence)
- **Employee experience tech (HR tech)**: $32 billion market globally (2022 estimate)
- **Contact center/CCaaS**: $18 billion market projected to reach $59 billion by 2028

Vendors positioning in the TX space include: ServiceNow (EX platform), Salesforce (CX/EX integration), Microsoft (Teams as TX hub), SAP (SuccessFactors + CX), Oracle (Fusion Cloud), and pure-play experience platforms like Medallia and Qualtrics.

#### 3.2 Core Framework: The Four Dimensions of TX

**Customer Experience (CX):**
How customers perceive interactions with a brand across all touchpoints—digital, physical, service. Metrics: NPS, CSAT, CES (Customer Effort Score).

**Employee Experience (EX):**
How employees feel about their work environment, tools, culture, and development. Metrics: eNPS, engagement scores, turnover rate, productivity.

**User Experience (UX):**
The design quality of specific digital interfaces—usability, accessibility, visual design. Metrics: task completion rate, error rate, time-on-task.

**Multiexperience (MX):**
The consistency and quality of interactions across modalities—mobile app, web browser, voice assistant, AR/VR. Metrics: cross-channel conversion, modality usage ratios.

TX succeeds when these four reinforce each other: well-designed tools (UX) make employees effective (EX), which enables better customer service (CX), delivered consistently across channels (MX).

#### 3.3 Industry Examples

**USAA (Financial Services)**: Long cited as TX exemplar—consistently ranks highest in customer satisfaction AND employee satisfaction. Their integrated design approach for member and employee digital tools drives this correlation.

**Marriott**: During COVID, Marriott redesigned both their guest app (MX) and employee communication tools simultaneously, treating them as a unified experience ecosystem. Guest satisfaction recovered faster than competitors.

**Deutsche Telekom**: Implemented a TX program connecting customer support agent tools with customer-facing chatbots—when agents had better tools (EX), customer resolution rates improved dramatically.

**Apple Retail**: The Apple Store experience is a TX case study—integrated physical/digital experience for customers, backed by employee "Genius" training and tools that create consistent, high-quality interactions.

#### 3.4 Implementation Challenges

**Organizational Silo Problem**: CX typically owned by Marketing/CTO, EX owned by HR, UX owned by Product/Design. No natural owner for TX. Creates political challenges.

**Technology Integration Complexity**: Most enterprises have separate CRM (customer data), HRIS (employee data), and analytics platforms—integrating these for TX insights is technically complex.

**Measurement Problem**: How do you measure the *connection* between employee and customer experience? Correlation analytics require longitudinal data and controlled variables.

**Culture Before Technology**: TX strategies often fail when companies buy technology (DXP, experience platforms) without changing organizational culture and incentive structures.

#### 3.5 Maturity Assessment

**2021 Status**: TX as a coherent strategy was nascent. Individual components (CX programs, EX initiatives) were mainstream; their integration was innovator-stage.

**2022-2024 Evolution**: TX became more discussed but less specifically labeled. Many organizations pursued its components without the "TX" branding. The concept matured into mainstream enterprise strategy discussions by 2023.

**Where It Is Now (2025)**: TX has largely dissolved into "employee experience" and "digital experience platform" conversations. The TX label faded, but the underlying insight—that EX and CX are connected—is now conventional wisdom.

---

### 4. Problem/Drivers

1. **Pandemic-driven simultaneity**: Had to fix employee AND customer digital experiences at the same time
2. **Digital talent shortage**: When good digital tools attract/retain employees, it becomes a competitive differentiator
3. **Customer expectation inflation**: Consumers expect Amazon/Apple-quality experiences everywhere
4. **Siloed investment inefficiency**: Separate CX and EX investments often duplicate infrastructure or create contradictions

---

### 5. Value Proposition

Organizations implementing TX report:
- Faster resolution of customer issues (empowered employees with better tools)
- Higher employee retention (better digital tools = better job satisfaction)
- Consistent brand experience across digital and physical touchpoints
- Reduced technology fragmentation costs through shared infrastructure

Gartner claimed organizations providing TX would outperform competitors across key satisfaction metrics over 3 years (2021-2024).

### 6. Unresolved Questions (End of Round 1)

1. Is TX a distinct strategic concept or just good CX management extended to employees?
2. What specific technology investments drive TX outcomes vs. just experience-washing?
3. How should TX programs be measured and who should own them organizationally?

## Round 2: Deep-Dive — The Organizational Ownership Problem

### Research Question

The most practical unresolved question: **Who should own TX in the enterprise, and how should TX programs be structured organizationally?**

This matters because TX fails most often not from technology gaps but from organizational misalignment.

### Deep Findings

#### The Ownership Vacuum

TX spans traditional organizational boundaries:
- **CMO** owns CX, customer data, brand
- **CHRO** owns EX, employee data, culture
- **CDO/CTO** owns technology platforms
- **CPO/VP Design** owns UX

No single C-suite leader has natural authority over all four TX dimensions. This creates:
- Budget fragmentation (TX investments split across functions)
- Incentive misalignment (CX metrics don't show up in HR dashboards)
- Technology duplication (CX platforms and EX platforms often don't share data)

#### Emerging Solutions

**Model 1: Chief Experience Officer (CXO)**
Some organizations create a CXO role encompassing customer, employee, and digital experience. Risk: authority without resources.

**Model 2: TX Governance Committee**
Cross-functional committee with representation from Marketing, HR, IT, and Product. Requires executive sponsorship to avoid becoming a talkshop.

**Model 3: Platform-Led Integration**
Use a unified technology platform (ServiceNow, Salesforce Experience Cloud) as the organizational glue—the platform forces integration. Risk: technology-first approach without strategic alignment.

**Model 4: Experience Center of Excellence**
Create a centralized design and strategy team that serves multiple functions. Successful at companies like IBM (IBM iX) and Accenture.

#### Recommendation Framework

For a mid-to-large enterprise (5,000+ employees) starting a TX program:

1. **Audit**: Map existing CX, EX, UX, MX initiatives and their owners
2. **Find the sponsor**: Identify a C-suite executive whose KPIs benefit from TX integration (often CEO or COO)
3. **Baseline measurement**: Establish NPS, eNPS, and digital tool satisfaction scores before TX investment
4. **Start small, connected**: Pick one customer journey that directly involves employee touchpoints—redesign together
5. **Measure the correlation**: Track whether EX improvements in the pilot area correlate with CX improvements
6. **Build the business case**: Use pilot data to justify broader TX investment

### Round 2 Conclusion

TX's biggest barrier is organizational, not technological. The technology components exist and are mature. The insight (EX → CX connection) is empirically supported. What's missing in most enterprises is a power structure and incentive system that rewards TX outcomes rather than siloed function optimization. Organizations that solve the ownership problem first will get dramatically better returns from TX technology investments.
